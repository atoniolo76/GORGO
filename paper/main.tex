\begin{abstract}
In cross-region LLM serving, time-to-first-token (TTFT) is highly sensitive to
per-request routing decisions. KV-cache reuse across replicas can eliminate much
of the prefill computation on multi-turn workloads, but realizing this potential
requires jointly balancing cache locality against replica load and network cost.
Additionally, public datasets to evaluate these policies do not represent
long-context, high prefix-reuse workloads: LMSYS and WildChat average 2 and 2.5
turns per conversation, 0.5 and 2.9k tokens/request, and 5 and 9\% intra-user
reuse, respectively. To capture the setting where a majority of requests contain
reusable prefixes, we evaluate common routing policies such as least-load,
prefix-aware, session-affinity, and others on traces from a production inference
endpoint featuring multi-turn conversations (${\sim}82$ requests/user),
7--45$\times$ longer prompts (21k avg tokens), and 2--6$\times$ higher KV-cache
reuse (55\%). We evaluate policies on a multi-region GPU cluster where
cross-region network latency ranges from 26ms to 466ms across replicas to test
GORGO, a policy that jointly optimizes cache reuse, network latency, and queue
depth with weights optimized online to minimize p95 TTFT with no offline
profiling. GORGO achieves the lowest TTFT at every percentile (p50, p95, p99)
across both a tuning window and a held-out evaluation window against all other
load balancing policies, highlighting the impact of online joint optimization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Modern LLM serving is increasingly cross-region. Operators distribute model
replicas across multiple geographies for three reasons: to follow diurnally
shifting demand from a globally distributed user base, to provide redundancy
against regional capacity loss or partition, and to aggregate accelerator
capacity that no single region currently supplies \citep{skywalker,skyserve}.
Once replicas span regions, the gateway that routes incoming requests
becomes a first-class component of TTFT, sitting between the client and the
inference engines and choosing one replica per request based on observable
state \citep{aibrix2024,nginx2024}.

Time-to-first-token on a single replica decomposes into three terms: the
network round-trip from the gateway to that replica, the queueing delay
behind in-flight requests admitted via continuous batching \citep{orca}, and
the prefill computation for whatever portion of the input prompt is not
already cached. Modern inference engines expose a per-replica radix-tree
KV-cache that lets incoming requests skip prefill for any prefix already
present on the handling replica \citep{sglang2024,vllm2023}. On long-context
multi-turn traffic that saving can be the difference between sub-second TTFT
and multi-second TTFT, so KV-cache locality at the routing layer is a
first-order determinant of latency \citep{skywalker,qin2024mooncake}.

These three TTFT terms pull in different directions. Routing to the
geographically nearest replica minimizes network RTT but ignores cache state;
routing to the most cache-warm replica maximizes prefix reuse but can pile
onto a saturated batch; routing to the least-loaded replica balances queue
depth but is cache- and locality-blind. Standard production heuristics
implement one of these signals at a time: NGINX-style least-request and
least-connections \citep{nginx2024}, AIBrix-style longest-prefix-match
\citep{aibrix2024}, hash-based session affinity, and so on. They suffice for
workloads with short prompts, weak prefix reuse, and uniform replica
latencies, but interactive long-context traffic breaks all three assumptions:
production chat traces concentrate most tokens in a small set of returning
users with substantial prefix overlap, prompts run to tens of thousands of
tokens, and cross-region RTTs differ by an order of magnitude. The right
routing target for a given request can change minute-to-minute as load shifts,
and weighting one signal in isolation leaves performance on the table at the
tail.

A second obstacle is empirical: the public chat datasets used in prior
LLM-serving evaluations underrepresent the regime where these trade-offs
matter. LMSYS-Chat-1M \citep{lmsys2024} and WildChat-4.8M \citep{wildchat2024}
average 470 and 2{,}900 tokens per request with intra-user prefix reuse below
10\%; the production endpoint we evaluate carries 21{,}000 tokens per request
on average and 53.7\% intra-user prefix reuse. Public datasets thus produce
TTFT distributions that are nearly indistinguishable across cache-aware and
cache-blind policies \citep{skywalker}, which masks routing differences that
production traffic exposes.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(i) A workload characterization (\S\ref{sec:dataset}) placing three datasets on
the same prefix-reuse and request-length axes; the GLM-5.1 production trace
has 7--45$\times$ longer prompts and 2--6$\times$ higher token-weighted reuse
than LMSYS-Chat-1M and WildChat-4.8M.
(ii) A routing cost model and three variants collectively called GORGO
(\S\ref{sec:method}) that score each replica by an additive combination of
EWMA network RTT, estimated prefill cost over uncached tokens, and projected
decode-rate cost over running and queued tokens. Variants differ only in how
the two per-token rate parameters are set: a static configuration, a
per-replica median-of-rates fit, or a Gaussian $(1{+}1)$-ES hill climb that
directly minimizes p95 TTFT.
(iii) An experimental setup (\S\ref{sec:setup}) running each of eight policies
on its own dedicated 2$\times$L40S three-replica fleet in Seoul, Frankfurt,
and Ashburn, with cross-region RTTs spanning 26--466\,ms.
(iv) Empirical results (\S\ref{sec:results}) across multiple 30-minute slices
of GLM-5.1 traffic on two consecutive days plus a higher-concurrency stress
window: in every window, two GORGO variants together cover the lowest TTFT
at p50, p95, and p99 against seven baseline policies; on a WildChat replay
the GORGO advantage collapses, confirming that the result is regime-dependent
on long-context, high-reuse traffic.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{KV caching and prefix reuse.}
Inference engines such as SGLang \citep{sglang2024} and vLLM \citep{vllm2023}
maintain a Key--Value cache of attention activations for every active sequence;
new tokens are appended to this cache step-by-step and admitted into the next
decoding iteration via continuous batching \citep{orca}. Beyond per-request
state, the same cache enables \emph{prefix reuse}: when an incoming request
shares a token-level prefix with a sequence already cached on a replica, that
prefix's KV state can be reused and prefill is skipped for those tokens.
SGLang's RadixAttention \citep{sglang2024} operationalizes
this via a per-replica radix trie that tracks which prefixes are resident; on
multi-turn workloads where the same user returns repeatedly, a replica that
has seen the conversation can serve the next turn at a fraction of the prefill
cost.

\textbf{Request-level routing as its own layer.}
Inside a single replica, a per-request scheduler decides admission and order
within the running batch \citep{sglang2024,orca}; across replicas, a
\emph{request-level router} (or gateway) decides which replica receives a
request in the first place. AIBrix \citep{aibrix2024} is a recent example of
such a routing layer designed for LLM gateways, exposing prefix-aware,
least-load, and least-request policies behind a common interface; production
gateways like NGINX \citep{nginx2024} and AWS Bedrock cross-region inference
\citep{aws_bedrock_xregion} occupy the same architectural slot but at the
network level, with no surface for KV state. Routing-layer decisions matter
disproportionately on long-context workloads because the prefill term they
implicitly select for can dominate TTFT \citep{skywalker,qin2024mooncake}.

\textbf{Routing-cost decomposition.}
For request $r$ routed to replica $u$, TTFT depends on three signal
families the gateway can observe, each of which decomposes further:
(1)~\emph{Network latency}: the proxy-to-replica RTT $\mathrm{rtt}(u)$
\citep{vulimiri2015}.
(2)~\emph{Replica load}, comprising (2a)~decode tokens of running
requests in the current batch, (2b)~prefill tokens of requests queued
ahead of $r$, and (2c)~$r$'s own prefill cost once admitted.
(3)~\emph{Available prefix cache}, comprising prefix overlap with
(3a)~the running batch (some KV state may already be evicted),
(3b)~queued requests (their KV state will exist by the time $r$ runs),
and (3c)~$r$'s own cached prior turns under the same conversation.
A routing policy is a function over these nine sub-signals; no production
policy we are aware of jointly weighs even all three top-level families.

\textbf{Common policies.}
We consider five non-GORGO baselines representative of production systems:
\textsc{random} (cache- and load-blind), \textsc{least-request} and
\textsc{least-load} (load-aware, cache-blind), \textsc{prefix-cache}
(longest-prefix-match with a least-request fallback under load imbalance, in
the AIBrix style \citep{aibrix2024}), and \textsc{simple-session-affinity}
(token-prefix hash to a fixed replica). \S\ref{sec:baselines} states their
exact form. None of the five takes more than one of the three signal
families above as a primary objective.

\textbf{Production cross-region gateways and prior systems.}
Industrial cross-region routing exists today but is opaque to KV state. AWS
Bedrock cross-region inference \citep{aws_bedrock_xregion} and GKE
multi-cluster Gateways \citep{gke_gateway} provide global routing and failover
but treat inference as an HTTP endpoint. Serverless GPU platforms such as
Modal \citep{modal_prefix_caching} support intra-region prefix caching but do
not optimize across regions; SkyPilot \citep{skypilot} aggregates capacity
across clouds but does not observe per-replica KV state. SkyServe
\citep{skyserve} manages cross-region placement and SkyWalker
\citep{skywalker} adds KV-aware cross-region load balancing with selective
KV pushing. GORGO occupies the gap between opaque gateways and KV-aware
single-region schedulers: a per-request, multi-region routing layer that
combines all three signal families on a single scalar cost score, with the
two scaling weights tuned online.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Public datasets sit in the wrong regime.}
Routing policies that exploit cache locality or user stickiness improve TTFT
by choosing a replica with high prefix-cache reuse, which we measure as the
prefix-hit rate---the fraction of an incoming request's input tokens that
match a previously-seen prefix on the chosen replica. On a corpus where
prefix-hit rates are uniformly low across all replicas, no cache-aware policy
has anywhere to win. This is the regime occupied by the public chat datasets:
LMSYS-Chat-1M \citep{lmsys2024} averages 467 input tokens per request and
9\% global token-weighted prefix reuse, and WildChat-4.8M \citep{wildchat2024}
averages 2{,}925 input tokens and 5.3\% intra-user reuse. The combination of
short prompts and weak reuse pushes prefill cost down enough that TTFT
distributions are nearly indistinguishable across cache-aware and cache-blind
policies---an observation also made for cross-region serving more broadly
\citep{skywalker}. Public benchmarks on these corpora consequently
underestimate the gain from joint cache--network--queue-aware routing.

\textbf{GLM-5.1 production trace.}
To measure routing in the regime where the trade-offs actually arise, we
compile a long-context, multi-turn chatbot dataset from production inference
traces of a commercial inference provider's free GLM-5.1 endpoint
\citep{glm2024}. The corpus contains
411{,}169 requests over 7 days from 4{,}984 distinct users, yielding $\sim$82
requests per user on average, with an average input length of 21{,}043 tokens
and 53.7\% intra-user token-weighted prefix reuse. Reuse is measured with a
token-weighted prefix trie that ingests each request's tokenized message
sequence in arrival order, partitioned by user, and reports intra-user
savings, cross-user (additional) savings, and global savings.
Table~\ref{tab:datasets} places the three datasets on the same axes; LMSYS
does not carry an IP or user identity, so we treat each conversation as a
separate user.

Three contrasts (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}, Fig.~\ref{fig:reuse_composition})
matter for routing. \textit{Request length}: GLM-5.1 prompts are 45$\times$
longer than LMSYS and 7$\times$ longer than WildChat---at the prefill rates we
measure, a 21{,}000-token uncached prompt costs seconds, not milliseconds.
\textit{User concentration}: GLM-5.1 has 82 requests per user; WildChat has
1.7; LMSYS has one. Intra-user reuse depends on returning users, which the
public sets do not have at scale. \textit{Reuse composition}: GLM-5.1's reuse
is intra-user multi-turn dialogue (53.7\% intra plus 1.6\% additional
cross-user), while WildChat's 34.4\% global is mostly cross-user shared
structure (5.3\% intra). On GLM-5.1, a policy that lands a returning user on
a replica that has seen their conversation can skip more than half of the
prefill; on the public datasets, the same policy has nothing to skip.

\textbf{Trace format and filtering.}
We re-encode GLM-5.1 into the Mooncake FAST'25 trace schema
\citep{qin2024mooncake} so that benchmarking is time-sequenced and reproducible:
each trace is a JSONL file in which every row carries an absolute-millisecond
timestamp, request metadata (input/output token counts, target model), and the
already-tokenized request body, which avoids exposing user data. Requests with
$>$24{,}000 input tokens are filtered out; this is short of the model's 32k
native context window to leave headroom for our tokenizer disagreeing with the
source tokenizer (so as to shrink the failed-request surface during testing).
The filtered trace is then chunked by absolute timestamp into 30-minute
windows; the windows we benchmark in \S\ref{sec:results} are drawn from
April 1 and April 2 of this trace at the same time-of-day band. Replay is
open-loop: requests are issued at the recorded arrival timestamps regardless
of in-flight load, exposing each policy to production burstiness rather than
to a closed-loop generator that would mask queueing pathologies.

\section{GORGO}
\label{sec:method}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Cost model.}
Following the decomposition in \S\ref{sec:background}, the GORGO score for
routing request $r$ to replica $u$ is the sum of the three TTFT terms the
gateway can observe directly:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{score}(u, r) = \mathrm{rtt}_u
  + t_{\mathrm{prefill}}(u) \cdot \bigl(|x_r| - |x_r \cap c_u|\bigr)
  + t_{\mathrm{decode}}(u) \cdot \bigl(\mathrm{queued\_tokens}_u + \mathrm{used\_kv\_tokens}_u\bigr),
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where $|x_r| - |x_r \cap c_u|$ is the count of uncached input tokens for $r$
on $u$, and $\mathrm{queued\_tokens}_u + \mathrm{used\_kv\_tokens}_u$ is the
combined token-weighted load already on $u$ (queued tokens awaiting admission
plus KV-cache footprint of running requests, both reported by SGLang).
$t_{\mathrm{prefill}}$ and $t_{\mathrm{decode}}$ are per-replica per-token rate
parameters with units of seconds-per-token: $t_{\mathrm{prefill}}$ converts
uncached input-token count into expected prefill delay, $t_{\mathrm{decode}}$
converts ahead-of-line token load into expected decode/queueing delay. The
proxy sends $r$ to the replica with the minimum score. The two parameters are
stored \emph{per replica}, with global defaults overridden on any replica
whose GPU class, batch size, or model variant differs; the online tuner
writes per-replica overrides automatically because each TTFT sample is
labeled with its destination. Three signals feed the score: (i)
$\mathrm{rtt}_u$ from an EWMA of a lightweight network probe decoupled from
the metrics scrape, (ii) $|x_r \cap c_u|$ from the proxy's local radix trie
of cached prefixes per replica, refreshed on every request completion, and
(iii) load from SGLang's per-replica metrics combined with the proxy's
in-flight token counter.

\textbf{Three weight-selection variants.}
The three GORGO variants share Equation~\ref{eq:score} and differ only in how
$t_{\mathrm{prefill}}$ and $t_{\mathrm{decode}}$ are chosen.

\textbf{gorgo-static} fixes both parameters at run start and never adjusts
them. The starter values can be hand-set or the result of an offline
characterization run on a previous window; the variant isolates the
contribution of the cost model independent of online adaptation.

\textbf{gorgo-autotune} re-fits both rates online by linear regression
through the origin on a trailing 64-sample window, partitioned by
destination replica, every 16 new samples. For $t_{\mathrm{prefill}}(u)$
we fit network-latency-normalized TTFT $(\mathrm{TTFT} - \mathrm{rtt}_u)$
as a linear function of uncached input tokens; for $t_{\mathrm{decode}}(u)$
we fit network-latency-normalized total completion time
$(\mathrm{TCT} - \mathrm{rtt}_u)$ as a linear function of completion
tokens, decode-phase only. Implementation note: the median of per-sample
rates we use is equivalent to a robust slope through the origin and tracks
per-replica hardware drift. The variant treats the cost-model weights as
identifiable physical rates rather than black-box knobs, which is also its
failure mode---the per-sample rate
$(\mathrm{TTFT} - \mathrm{rtt})/\mathrm{uncached}$ inflates when uncached
counts are small (high cache hit), so the median drifts upward.

\textbf{gorgo-hillclimb} treats the two parameters as black-box knobs and runs
a Gaussian $(1{+}1)$-evolution strategy \citep{rechenberg1973} over them in
log-space, with the negative p95 TTFT of a rolling 64-request window as the
objective. Every 16 new samples the tuner scores the current incumbent,
accepts or rejects the most recent candidate, and proposes a new candidate
$\theta_{\mathrm{new}} = \exp\bigl(\log\theta_{\mathrm{best}} + \sigma\,\mathcal{N}(0, I)\bigr)$.
The step size $\sigma$ adapts according to Rechenberg's 1/5-success rule
\citep{rechenberg1973}: of the last 5 candidates evaluated, roughly one
should improve the incumbent. We track the acceptance rate $m$ over a
trailing window and adjust $\sigma$ multiplicatively---decreasing it (factor
$\sim$0.82) when $m > 1/5$ (the search is too tight to make progress) and
increasing it (factor $\sim$1/0.82) when $m < 1/5$ (the search is too loose
and rarely finds improvements). The 1/5 rule is what makes online tuning practical at our timescales:
matching empirical acceptance to 1/5 keeps the proposal scale calibrated
for fast escape from poor regions early and refinement near the best-found
incumbent late. On a 30-minute window with a 64-sample cohort and
16-sample hop, this drives the search to within a few percent of the
best-found incumbent in roughly a hundred candidate evaluations
(Fig.~\ref{fig:cache_convergence}b).

\section{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

We compare GORGO against five baseline policies.

\textbf{random} samples a replica uniformly per request; it is the lower bound.

\textbf{least-request} routes to the replica with the fewest in-flight requests,
scoring by $\max(\mathrm{sgLang\_running\_reqs}_u, \mathrm{proxy\_inflight}_u)$
to bridge the $\sim$10\,s staleness between metrics scrapes \citep{nginx2024}.

\textbf{least-load} routes to the replica with the lowest aggregate
token-weighted load: $\mathrm{running} + \mathrm{queued} +
\mathrm{used\_kv\_tokens} + \mathrm{queued\_tokens}_u$. It is heavier-signal
than least-request but cache-blind.

\textbf{prefix-cache} is the AIBrix-style longest-prefix-match policy
\citep{aibrix2024}. It picks the replica with the longest cached prefix match
and falls back to least-request when the running-request imbalance exceeds a
soft threshold.

\textbf{simple-session-affinity} hashes the first 256 token IDs of the prompt
modulo the number of replicas. The same prefix always maps to the same replica,
maximizing intra-user reuse but providing no load-escape mechanism.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Hardware and software.}
Each policy runs on its own dedicated three-replica fleet so routing
decisions for one policy cannot warm or cool another policy's caches. Each
fleet consists of one 2$\times$L40S SGLang \citep{sglang2024} server
(tensor-parallel 2, 96\,GB VRAM per replica) in each of Asia (Seoul,
\texttt{ap-seoul}), Europe (Frankfurt, \texttt{eu-frankfurt}), and US East
(Ashburn, \texttt{us-ashburn}), serving Qwen3.5-35B-A3B-FP8
\citep{qwen3technical}. A CPU proxy in US East hosts the routing policy and
replays the trace. The eight policies (random, least-request, least-load,
prefix-cache, simple-session-affinity, and the three GORGO variants) are
benchmarked simultaneously on disjoint fleets so that any cross-region
network variance during a window applies equally to all of them; GPUs are
not shared across policies.

The proxy scrapes Prometheus metrics every $\sim$10\,s and runs a separate
EWMA-smoothed HTTP probe for per-replica RTT, decoupled from the metrics
scrape to avoid handler contention. Before each run a homogeneity check
issues 16 streaming requests directly to each replica; runs whose
worst-to-median TTFT ratio exceeds 3$\times$ are flagged (none occurred).

\textbf{Windows.}
We benchmark across multiple 30-minute slices of the GLM-5.1 Mooncake trace
covering April 1 and April 2, plus a higher-concurrency stress slice on
April 2:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}\setlength\topsep{0pt}
\item \textbf{W1 (April 1, 00:30--01:00 UTC, $c{=}32$):} tuning window.
  GORGO variants seed from manually-chosen starter weights and the online
  tuners adapt during the window.
\item \textbf{W2 (April 2, 00:30--01:00 UTC, $c{=}32$):} cross-day evaluation
  window. Same time-of-day band as W1, different day. \texttt{gorgo-static}
  deploys the per-replica weights that \texttt{gorgo-hillclimb} learned in
  W1 and never adjusts them; the other GORGO variants continue tuning.
\item \textbf{Stress (April 2, 00:30--01:00 UTC, $c{=}64$):} same trace as
  W2 replayed at concurrency 64 to test behavior under elevated load.
\end{itemize}
Concurrency $c$ is the in-flight-request cap at the proxy. Output is capped
at 128 tokens per request so TTFT, not decode, dominates the latency budget.

\textbf{Cross-region RTT.}
Probed from the US East proxy with a dedicated lightweight HTTP probe over
the duration of W1, mean EWMA-smoothed RTT is 25.6$\pm$3.8\,ms to the local
US East replica, 232$\pm$72\,ms to Frankfurt, and 466$\pm$138\,ms to
Seoul; the high coefficient of variation on the cross-region legs (29--31\%)
reflects routing-table churn during the window.
Figure~\ref{fig:rtt} plots the time series.

percentiles but counted in success rate.

\textbf{Regime sensitivity (WildChat replay).}
A control replay on WildChat-4.8M with the same fleet and proxy reverses the
ranking: \texttt{simple-session-affinity} wins p95 (1.025\,s) and p99
(1.712\,s), \texttt{least-request} is competitive (1.036\,s p95), and the
GORGO variants are between 30\% and 250\% worse on p95
(\texttt{gorgo-hillclimb} 1.325\,s, \texttt{gorgo-static} 1.932\,s,
\texttt{gorgo-autotune} 3.583\,s). On a corpus where prompts average
2{,}900 tokens and intra-user reuse is 5.3\%, the prefill term that GORGO's
cost model is most sensitive to is small relative to network and queue
terms, and the per-token rate parameters do not converge during a 30-minute
window. The advantage GORGO shows on GLM-5.1 is therefore regime-dependent
on long-context, high-reuse traffic; we discuss this in
\S\ref{sec:limitations}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Statistical scope.}
Three 30-minute windows from one production trace; each policy run once
per window. With ${\sim}2{,}500$--$4{,}800$ successful responses, p99
standard error is $\sigma_{\mathrm{TTFT}}/\sqrt{n\cdot 0.01\cdot 0.99}
\approx 0.05$--$0.07$\,s. GORGO p95/p99 wins are several SE clear of the
next baseline in W2 and Stress; in W1 the p99 lead is $\sim$1\,SE.
Cross-window replication on additional time-of-day slices remains future
work.
\textbf{Regime sensitivity.}
The WildChat replay (\S\ref{sec:results}) shows GORGO's advantage is
specific to long-context, high-reuse traffic: on prompts averaging
2{,}900 tokens with 5.3\% intra-user reuse, the prefill term is small,
the per-token rates do not converge in 30 minutes, and
\texttt{simple-session-affinity} wins. We claim improvement in the regime
characterized in \S\ref{sec:dataset}, not universally.
\textbf{Methodological and operational caveats.}
\texttt{gorgo-autotune} is the weakest GORGO variant on GLM-5.1 because the
per-sample rate $({\rm TTFT}-{\rm rtt})/\mathrm{uncached}$ inflates at high
cache hit, biasing the fit. \texttt{gorgo-hillclimb} requires several hop
cycles to converge; this fits comfortably in 30-minute windows but not in
arbitrarily short ones. Each policy ran on a single-tenant three-replica
fleet; the centralized proxy is a single point of failure, and a
distributed peer-summary GORGO variant is sketched but not benchmarked.
The fleet uses homogeneous GPUs and does not split prefill/decode
\citep{distserve2024,splitwise2024}, which would require an additional
routing term; GORGO uses only HTTP-exposed metrics, not scheduler-internal
state \citep{kvflow2025,lpc2025} that could tighten the prefill estimate.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Prefix caches and reuse.}
RadixAttention in SGLang \citep{sglang2024} stores per-request KV state in a
radix tree; PagedAttention in vLLM \citep{vllm2023} enables prefix sharing
through paged memory. KVLink \citep{kvlink2025}, ChunkKV \citep{chunkkv2025},
KVFlow \citep{kvflow2025}, and Learned Prefix Caching \citep{lpc2025} improve
the single-replica cache but do not decide which replica serves a request.
\textbf{Routing and scheduling.}
Mooncake \citep{qin2024mooncake} is a KV-cache-centric architecture and the
source of our trace format. AIBrix \citep{aibrix2024} provides prefix-aware
load balancing as a baseline; k-LPM \citep{klpm2025} formulates LLM scheduling
under TTFT constraints as NP-hard; DLPM \citep{dlpm2025} targets fairness with
locality; Llumnix \citep{llumnix2024} migrates requests across replicas;
Multi-LLM routers \citep{routerwu2025} address model selection; CacheBlend
\citep{yao2024cacheblend} routes by trie-measured prefix length but does not
measure network RTT. GORGO commits to a single scalar score per replica with
online weight tuning and requires no engine modifications.
\textbf{Online and learned routing.}
Performance-aware LLM load balancing \citep{jain2025performance} learns a
routing policy over mixed prefill/decode workloads; bandit and RL schedulers
\citep{decima} and Bayesian-optimization configuration tuners
\citep{cherrypick} explore the broader space. GORGO keeps the policy
interpretable (an additive cost) and applies $(1{+}1)$-ES only to two
scaling weights, with TTFT as direct reward.
\textbf{PD-disaggregation.}
Sarathi-Serve \citep{sarathi_serve}, DistServe \citep{distserve2024}, and
Splitwise \citep{splitwise2024} disaggregate prefill from decode at the batch
or GPU-pool level. These reduce the compute component of TTFT but do not
resolve geo-routing trade-offs when locality and network latency conflict;
GORGO is complementary and assumes an underlying stack that may already use
either.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

We characterize a workload regime---long-context, multi-turn, high
prefix-reuse production traffic---where standard LLM-serving routing
heuristics leave performance on the table because they optimize one of
network RTT, replica load, or cache locality and ignore the other two. On a
411{,}000-request production trace where 53.7\% of input tokens are part of
a previously-seen prefix from the same user, GORGO---a single-score additive
cost model with two per-token rate parameters tuned online---collectively
achieves the lowest TTFT at every percentile (p50, p95, p99) across a
tuning window, a cross-day evaluation window, and a higher-concurrency
stress window on a 2$\times$L40S three-region fleet with cross-region RTTs
spanning 26--466\,ms. Per-percentile wins are not held by any single fixed
weight setting: \texttt{gorgo-hillclimb}'s online (1+1)-ES dominates during
tuning, the W1-learned weights frozen into \texttt{gorgo-static} dominate
under cross-day evaluation, and the two together cover all percentiles
under stress. A WildChat replay demonstrates that the result is
regime-dependent on the long-context, high-reuse axis, not a universal win.
The cost model and tuner fit on a stock HTTP gateway and require no offline
profiling.
