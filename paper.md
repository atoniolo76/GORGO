# GORGO: Gaussian-Optimized Request-level Routing for Generative Orchestration

## Abstract

Intelligent per-request routing is the largest untapped lever for reducing time-to-first-token (TTFT) in multi-region LLM serving, where cross-region network latency alone can exceed 200ms per hop. KV-cache reuse across replicas can eliminate over half of prefill computation on multi-turn workloads, but realizing this potential requires jointly balancing cache locality against replica load and network cost — policies that optimize cache hits alone, such as session-affinity and prefix-aware routing, concentrate traffic on cache-warm replicas and degrade tail latency through load skew. We present the first systematic evaluation of request-routing policies for LLM inference gateways, benchmarking least-load, least-request, prefix-aware, session-affinity, and other strategies across three regions (Asia, Europe, US East) serving Qwen3.5-35B on isolated per-policy fleets. Since public chat datasets have short prompts with low prefix reuse (LMSYS and WildChat average 2–2.5 turns per conversation, 0.5–2.9k tokens/request, and 5–9% intra-user reuse), we evaluate on traces from a production GLM-5.1 endpoint featuring multi-turn conversations (~82 requests/user), 7–45× longer prompts (21k avg tokens), and 2–6× higher KV-cache reuse (55%) — a regime where the tension between cache locality and load balance directly determines TTFT. We show that existing policies each reduce routing to a single brittle heuristic and fail predictably when it is not the binding constraint. From these failure modes we derive GORGO, a policy that jointly optimizes across cache reuse, network latency, and queue depth via an additive cost model, with weights calibrated online by a Gaussian (1+1)-Evolution Strategy that directly minimizes observed p95 TTFT — requiring no offline profiling. On a held-out 30-minute evaluation window (~4.2k requests per policy), GORGO achieves the lowest TTFT at every percentile (p50–p99): 1.510s p95 versus 1.757s for least-request, 1.632s for prefix-cache, 1.763s for random, and 2.080s for session-affinity. Versus session-affinity — the strongest cache-aware baseline — GORGO improves median TTFT by 26%, reduces p99 by 47%, routes 16× more evenly across replicas, and degrades 6–9× less when user patterns shift between time windows. Hyperparameters learned from 30 minutes of traffic generalize without retuning. Code, traces, and experiment specs are available at https://github.com/Arcadia-Research-Team/GORGO.
